Memorae · Product design challenge

Four features, one question: what needs me now?

Redesigning The Office — Memorae's actionable memory space — from a dashboard you browse into a space you act from.

Role · Product Designer Scope · Mobile + Web Time · ~6 hours
The problem

Four features, four cognitive modes, one flat dashboard.

The Office surfaces Reminders, Tasks, Calendar, and Weekly Briefing as four separate tiles — each asking the user to switch mental gears. The result is a dashboard you browse, not one you act from.

The thesis

These aren't four features. They're one question.

To the user, a reminder, a task, and an event are the same thing: something that needs attention at some point in time. So the home stops being four tiles and collapses into two time-based zones.

One scope call shaped this: I left Lists out. The brief defines The Office as time-bound, execution memory and The Park as ambient, no-urgency memory — by that line, a grocery list lives in the Park, not here, even though the current product files it alongside reminders.

Today — what to act on now. This week — what's coming. Everything merged by time, not by feature.

The Office home — Today and This week, merged by time.

Three decisions

What shaped the design.

1

Time over category

Reminders, tasks, and events merge into one chronological list, kept legible by icon. Hierarchy comes from urgency — not from feature boundaries.

2

Trust through completeness

The job isn't "show me today" — it's "show me nothing slipped." Overdue items escalate with specific labels; completed items stay visible, struck through, as proof.

3

Creation that feels like texting

One input parses natural language and routes it to the right type — no forms, no choosing a category first. It confirms how it understood you, with an auto-dismiss and undo.

Type naturally → Memorae parses it → confirm, with an auto-dismiss window.

Scaling invisibly

From three reminders to two hundred items — same structure.

An empty state invites a first action instead of showing hollow cards. A heavy day still surfaces only the top items, with the rest one tap away. It never feels empty for new users, or overwhelming for power users.

Empty state invites a first action; the full day groups by time and shows what's done.

The deep-dive · Weekly Briefing

The feature that didn't exist yet — designed from scratch.

A workload heat map you can tap into, day by day, paired with action cards. Memorae doesn't just store your week — it reads it, and surfaces three kinds of thing:

Problem

Wednesday's overloaded

Nine items stacked between 9 and 2. Want to rebalance?

Gap

3 reminders, no time set

They won't notify you until you give them a time.

Moment

Two free evenings

Thursday and Friday after 6 are clear. Nice.

Tap any day in the heat map to see it; the action cards sit alongside.

The bet

If The Office becomes genuinely seamless, the app stops being a thing you check — and becomes the thing you trust more than WhatsApp. Because WhatsApp never shows you the whole picture. This does.

What I'd do next

What's unfinished, and on purpose.

  • Validate the auto-confirm window. Committing on a timer is fast — but I'd test whether the silent dismiss feels reassuring or risky for a misread date.
  • Pressure-test the status line. The "alive" feeling rests on one personalized sentence. It only works if it's well-written every day — that needs real content rules.
  • Extend action cards beyond the Briefing. The "Memorae noticed" pattern could surface in Reminders and Calendar too — one idea, reaching across features.
  • Push the desktop layout further. Same hierarchy, more room — let Today and the week breathe side by side rather than stack.
